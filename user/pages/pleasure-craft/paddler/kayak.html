Legally Required Safety Equipment

PFD or Lifejacket

There must be 1 of suitable size for each person on board and each needs to be approved for use in Canada.

Buoyant Heaving Line

One buoyant heaving line at least 15m (49’3”) long.

Bailer/Manual Bilge Pump

One bailer or manual bilge pump is required for kayaks under 6m in length.

Navigation Lights

This must be a white light, visible over 360° used between sundown and sunrise or when visibility is reduced due to fog, falling snow, etc.

Sound-signalling Device

One sound-signalling device or appliance. This could include a pealess whistle, a foghorn or a compressed air foghorn.

Watertight Flashlight 1

One watertight flashlight that is in working order and has properly functioning batteries.

Flares 1

Six flares of Type A, B or C.

Magnetic Compass 2

One magnetic compass is a good idea to carry if the vessel is being operated in the absence of navigation markers.

Radar Reflector 2

Radar reflectors are required for all boats under 20 metres and boats that are built of mostly non-metallic materials.

1 - Only required if vessel is 6 - 8 metres in length.
2 - Only required under certain conditions.

Rules of the Road

As lesser manoeuvrable craft than powerboats, PWCs or Sailboats, Kayaks are most often considered the Stand-On vessels. Therefore, when encountering boats of greater manoeuvrability, maintain your course and speed to enable the give-way boat to take appropriate action.

Due to the susceptibility of kayaks to wakes from larger vessels, it is advisable to stay well clear of these larger craft, where possible. Further, should you have to cross primary shipping channels, action should be taken to cross at right angles to expedite the crossing and minimize the danger.

Aids to Navigation

Aids to Navigation do not always apply to kayakers due to their vessels shallow draft, small size and ability to hug the shorelines. However, it is important for kayakers to have an understanding of the different types of Aids to Navigation so they are aware of how other powered vessels and vessels under sail will be manoeuvring around buoys and restriction signs.

Aids to Navigation are any type of maker that assists boaters in their safe navigation of waterways. These aids to navigation can be broken down into two types: Buoys, which are makers on the water and Day Beacons, which are markers on land. They can each come in a variety of shapes, sizes and colours. Furthermore, some may be illuminated to make them easier to see when visibility is limited. Navigation charts will indicate if a specific aid is illuminated along with the light’s colour and flashing pattern. Restriction signs are other types of navigational aids that inform each boater of certain laws and restrictions that pertain to various waterways.

Signalling for Help

When it comes to signalling your need for assistance, a key factor that comes into play is how far away you are from those whom you will need to communicate with. Some pieces of signalling equipment are designed for short distance signalling (where help is within visual/audible range), others for medium distance signalling (where help is just outside the visual range) while still others for long distance signalling (where help is outside reasonable visual range.) You should, therefore, ensure that you have the signalling capabilities best suited to the maximum distance you may encounter within your boating environment.

Because of the compact nature of kayaks, it’s not easy to carry a lot of signalling equipment. At a minimum, the Small Vessel Regulations mandate that the following be carried on board.

  • Either a watertight flashlight OR 6 flares of types A, B or C must be carried on board if the overall length of the craft is greater than 6 meters. If possible to carry both, the more equipment, the better.
  • A sound signalling device or appliance.
